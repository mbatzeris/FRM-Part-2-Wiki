What is the approximate hazard rate (default intensity) formula from credit spreads?	λ ≈ s / (1 − R), where s = credit spread over risk-free rate, R = recovery rate. Intuition: the spread is the market's "danger pay"; dividing by (1−R) converts it to per-year default probability.
A 2-year corporate bond yields 190 bp over risk-free; recovery rate is 35%. What is the average hazard rate?	λ = 0.0190 / (1 − 0.35) = 0.0190 / 0.65 = 2.92% per year.
What is the conditional PD in year n, given survival through year n−1?	Conditional PD(n) = Unconditional PD(n) / Survival Probability(n−1). It answers: "given we made it this far, what's the chance of defaulting this year?"
What happens to the hazard rate if recovery rate increases (for a given spread)?	Hazard rate DECREASES. Higher recovery means each default hurts less, so the same spread implies fewer defaults. λ = s/(1−R): as R↑, denominator↑, λ↓.
What are risk-neutral PDs used for?	PRICING instruments and valuing credit derivatives. They are implied by market prices (bond spreads, CDS). Think: "the market's nervous number (Q)."
What are real-world (physical) PDs used for?	SCENARIO ANALYSIS and future loss estimation. They come from historical rating-agency data. Think: "history's calmer number (P)."
Are risk-neutral PDs higher or lower than real-world PDs? Why?	Risk-neutral PDs are systematically HIGHER. Reason: defaults are not independent — systematic risk cannot be diversified, so investors demand extra yield (a "crash insurance premium").
TRAP: "Real-world PDs should be used for pricing credit derivatives." True or false?	FALSE. Pricing requires risk-neutral PDs because market prices already embed the risk premium. Using real-world PDs will systematically UNDERPRICE default risk.
Under an ISDA Master Agreement, when does the non-defaulting party become an unsecured creditor? (Two situations)	(1) Position value is POSITIVE and EXCEEDS the defaulter's posted collateral (loss = value − collateral). (2) Position value is NEGATIVE but smaller than the collateral YOU posted (loss = your collateral − |value|, i.e., excess collateral trapped in the defaulter's estate).
Company B defaults owing Company A $50,000; B posted only $30,000 collateral. What is A's unsecured claim?	$20,000 (= $50,000 − $30,000). The collateral shield only protects the first $30k.
What is the ISDA Master Agreement's role in bilateral derivatives?	It governs bilaterally cleared derivatives, specifying initial and variation margin requirements. On default, it defines close-out netting and collateral rights.
What is CVA?	Credit Value Adjustment = present value of the expected cost to the BANK if the COUNTERPARTY defaults. It is ALWAYS a cost (subtracted from derivative value).
What is DVA?	Debt Value Adjustment = present value of the expected cost to the COUNTERPARTY if the BANK defaults. Paradoxically, it is a BENEFIT to the bank (added to derivative value) because bank-default releases it from obligations.
What is the formula for credit-adjusted derivative value?	V(credit-adjusted) = V(no-default) − CVA + DVA. CVA subtracts (cost to you); DVA adds (benefit from your own default possibility).
SIGN TRAP: In V = V(no-default) − CVA + DVA, which sign does CVA get and which does DVA get?	CVA is SUBTRACTED (it's a cost). DVA is ADDED (it's a benefit). Mnemonic: "C costs, D donates."
What is wrong-way risk (WWR)?	PD is POSITIVELY correlated with exposure. Bad: when counterparty is most likely to default, your exposure to them is highest. Standard CVA models that assume independence will UNDERESTIMATE CVA.
What is right-way risk (RWR)?	PD is NEGATIVELY correlated with exposure. Good: when counterparty's default probability rises, your exposure to them naturally falls.
A new trade is positively correlated with existing trades. What happens to CVA and DVA?	Both CVA and DVA INCREASE. The new trade piles exposure onto the same counterparty at the same time, increasing expected default cost on both sides.
What are the three standard credit risk mitigants for bilaterally cleared transactions?	(1) NETTING — aggregate positive/negative MtMs into single net exposure. (2) COLLATERAL agreements — posted cash/securities kept on default. (3) DOWNGRADE TRIGGERS — contractual rights activated if counterparty rating falls below threshold.
Three trades with a single counterparty: +$12M, −$4M, −$3M. What is the net exposure after netting?	Gross exposure = $12M (only positive). Netted exposure = $12M − $4M − $3M = $5M. Exposure reduction = $7M.
Why do downgrade triggers lose effectiveness in a broad credit deterioration?	When many counterparties hit trigger levels simultaneously (like AIG 2008), everyone tries to exit at the same time. The "early exit" option becomes worthless exactly when you need it most.
What are the three sources of default correlation?	(1) External sector/region shocks. (2) Economy-wide cycles. (3) CONTAGION — one firm's default triggering another's.
Compare reduced-form vs structural models for default correlation.	REDUCED-FORM: hazard rates follow correlated stochastic processes driven by macro variables. Mathematically simple, reflect cycles well, but produce LOW achievable correlations. STRUCTURAL (Merton-like): correlated asset processes. Produce ARBITRARILY HIGH correlations but are COMPUTATIONALLY HEAVY.
TRAP: "Reduced-form models allow high default correlations." True or false?	FALSE. Reduced-form models have a LOW correlation ceiling (two firms defaulting in the same short window is unlikely). STRUCTURAL models allow arbitrarily high correlations.
What does the Gaussian copula transform into joint normal variables?	TIMES TO DEFAULT (τᵢ). NOT PDs, NOT recovery rates, NOT spreads. It maps each firm's non-normal time-to-default into a standard normal variable via percentile-to-percentile mapping.
What is the one-factor Gaussian copula decomposition?	xᵢ = √ρ × F + √(1−ρ) × Zᵢ, where F = common market factor, Zᵢ = firm-specific shock, ρ = copula correlation. Conditional on F, defaults become independent.
What is the conditional default probability formula in the one-factor copula?	Q(T|F) = N( [N⁻¹(Q(T)) − √ρ × F] / √(1−ρ) ), where N = standard normal CDF, Q(T) = marginal PD, ρ = copula correlation, F = common factor realization.
What is the Credit VaR formula using the Gaussian copula?	Credit VaR = L × (1 − RR) × V(T,X), where L = portfolio value, (1−RR) = LGD, V(T,X) = worst-case default rate percentile. Mnemonic: "portfolio × LGD × default-rate percentile."
Loan portfolio L = $100M, recovery RR = 40%, V(T,X) = 5%. What is the Credit VaR?	Credit VaR = $100M × (1 − 0.40) × 0.05 = $100M × 0.60 × 0.05 = $3M.
How does CreditMetrics differ from the basic Gaussian copula Credit VaR approach?	CreditMetrics runs Monte Carlo on credit-rating TRANSITIONS (not just defaults) via a transition matrix. Joint rating changes are linked by a Gaussian copula where copula correlations equal EQUITY-return correlations (not bond, not asset correlations).
