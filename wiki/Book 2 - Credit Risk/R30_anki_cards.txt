What is the approximate hazard rate (default intensity) formula from credit spreads?	λ ≈ s / (1 − R). --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: A bondholder earns spread s as compensation for bearing default risk. If default occurs, the bondholder loses (1 − R) per dollar of face value. So the spread "per unit of loss" gives the implied default rate. Rearranging: λ = s / (1 − R). --- KEY INSIGHT: The formula assumes a risk-neutral world where the spread exactly compensates expected loss. It is a first-order approximation — accurate for small PDs, less so for distressed names.
A 2-year corporate bond yields 190 bp over risk-free; recovery rate is 35%. What is the average hazard rate?	λ = 0.0190 / (1 − 0.35) = 0.0190 / 0.65 ≈ 2.92% per year. --- STEP-BY-STEP: (1) Spread s = 190 bp = 1.90%. (2) Loss given default = 1 − R = 1 − 0.35 = 0.65. (3) Hazard rate = spread / LGD = 0.019 / 0.65 = 0.0292. --- SANITY CHECK: The hazard rate (2.92%) is HIGHER than the raw spread (1.90%) because each default only causes a 65-cent loss per dollar — you need more defaults to justify the spread.
What is the conditional PD in year n, given survival through year n−1?	Conditional PD(n) = Unconditional PD(n) / Survival Probability(n−1). --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: Unconditional PD(n) is the probability at time 0 of defaulting in year n. But if we already know the firm survived to n−1, we must rescale by dividing out that survival probability. This is Bayes' rule: P(default in n | survived to n−1) = P(default in n AND survived to n−1) / P(survived to n−1). --- EXAM TRAP: Don't confuse unconditional PD (joint probability from time 0) with conditional PD (updated probability given survival). Conditional PD is always ≥ unconditional PD because the denominator ≤ 1.
What happens to the hazard rate if recovery rate increases (for a given spread)?	Hazard rate INCREASES. --- FORMULA: λ = s/(1−R). As R↑, denominator (1−R)↓, so λ↑. --- CAUSAL CHAIN: Higher recovery → each default costs less (lower LGD) → to justify the SAME spread (which equals λ × LGD), you need MORE defaults → hazard rate rises. --- INTUITION: The spread is fixed revenue to the bondholder. If each "accident" is less severe (high recovery), there must be more accidents to explain why the market charges that premium. --- EXAM TRAP: This is counterintuitive. Students expect higher recovery = "safer" = lower PD. But for a FIXED spread, it's the opposite. The spread is already set by the market — you're reverse-engineering what PD is implied.
What are risk-neutral PDs used for?	PRICING credit instruments (bonds, CDS, CVA). --- WHY: Market prices embed a risk premium above expected loss. Risk-neutral PDs absorb this premium into an inflated default probability, so you can discount at the risk-free rate and still get the correct market price. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: In a risk-neutral world, all assets earn the risk-free rate. To make this work mathematically, you increase PD until expected payoff (discounted at rf) = observed market price. This is why Q-measure PDs > P-measure PDs. --- SOURCE: Implied from CDS spreads or bond spreads via λ = s/(1−R).
What are real-world (physical) PDs used for?	SCENARIO ANALYSIS, economic capital, and internal risk management. --- WHY NOT PRICING: Real-world PDs reflect historical average default frequencies — they don't include the systematic risk premium investors demand. Using them for pricing would underprice credit risk. --- SOURCE: Rating agency transition matrices (e.g., Moody's: "5-year cumulative default rate for BBB = X%"). --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: P-measure PDs answer "how often does default actually happen?" Q-measure PDs answer "what PD makes the math price match the market?" These are fundamentally different questions.
Are risk-neutral PDs higher or lower than real-world PDs? Why?	Risk-neutral PDs are systematically HIGHER than real-world PDs. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC (3 layers): (1) Default risk has a systematic component — defaults cluster in recessions when investors' marginal utility is high. (2) Investors demand extra compensation for bearing this undiversifiable systematic default risk. (3) This "excess spread" above actuarial expected loss gets absorbed into a higher PD in the risk-neutral measure. --- ANALOGY: Like buying car insurance — the premium (Q-measure) exceeds your actual expected loss (P-measure) because the insurer charges for bearing the risk. --- MAGNITUDE: For investment-grade bonds, Q-PD can be 2-5x the P-PD. The gap narrows for high-yield bonds.
TRAP: "Real-world PDs should be used for pricing credit derivatives." True or false?	FALSE. --- WHY IT'S WRONG: Pricing requires risk-neutral PDs because market prices already embed a risk premium. If you use real-world (historical) PDs for pricing, you will systematically UNDERPRICE the instrument — your model says the CDS is cheap, but the market has already priced in extra compensation for systematic risk. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: Pricing = replication argument. You replicate payoffs and discount at risk-free rate, which requires Q-measure. Scenario analysis = forecasting actual losses, which requires P-measure. --- MNEMONIC: Pricing → Q (market Quote). Planning → P (Physical reality).
Under an ISDA Master Agreement, when does the non-defaulting party become an unsecured creditor? (Two situations)	TWO LOSS SCENARIOS: --- SCENARIO 1 (owed money, under-collateralized): Your position value is POSITIVE (counterparty owes you), but their posted collateral is LESS than what they owe. Loss = Value − Collateral. Logic: you seize the collateral but remain short on what's owed. The gap is an unsecured claim in bankruptcy. --- SCENARIO 2 (owe money, over-collateralized): Your position value is NEGATIVE (you owe them), but you posted MORE collateral than the obligation. Loss = Your Collateral − |Obligation|. Logic: your excess collateral is trapped in the defaulter's estate. You become an unsecured creditor for that trapped amount. --- KEY INSIGHT: Both scenarios are about a MISMATCH between collateral and exposure. The ISDA close-out netting only helps if collateral fully covers the gap.
Company B defaults owing Company A $50,000; B posted only $30,000 collateral. What is A's unsecured claim?	$20,000 = $50,000 − $30,000. --- STEP-BY-STEP: (1) A's exposure = $50,000 (B owes A). (2) Collateral posted by B = $30,000. (3) A seizes the $30,000 immediately under the ISDA close-out. (4) Remaining $20,000 becomes an unsecured claim in B's bankruptcy. --- RECOVERY IMPLICATIONS: As an unsecured creditor, A may recover only cents on the dollar for the $20k (historical average ~40%). So actual loss ≈ $20,000 × (1 − 0.40) = $12,000. --- LESSON: Collateral reduces but does NOT eliminate credit risk. The residual gap is the economic motivation for CVA.
What is the ISDA Master Agreement's role in bilateral derivatives?	The ISDA Master Agreement is the LEGAL BACKBONE of OTC bilateral derivatives. --- THREE CORE FUNCTIONS: (1) CLOSE-OUT NETTING: on default, all trades with the counterparty are terminated and netted into a single amount owed. Without this, each trade would be a separate bankruptcy claim. (2) COLLATERAL (CSA): the Credit Support Annex defines margin rules — initial margin, variation margin, eligible collateral, thresholds, and minimum transfer amounts. (3) EVENTS OF DEFAULT: defines what constitutes default (failure to pay, bankruptcy, cross-default) and triggers close-out. --- WHY IT MATTERS: Without ISDA netting, gross exposure could be 10-50x net exposure. The agreement is what makes bilateral OTC trading economically viable.
What is CVA?	CVA = Credit Value Adjustment = the present value of expected loss due to COUNTERPARTY default. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: (1) You hold a derivative worth V with a counterparty. (2) If the counterparty defaults, you lose some or all of V's positive value. (3) CVA quantifies this expected loss across all future scenarios and time periods. (4) It is ALWAYS subtracted from the risk-free value because counterparty default risk makes the derivative worth LESS to you. --- FORMULA INTUITION: CVA ≈ Σ over time periods of [PD(t) × Discounted Expected Exposure(t) × LGD]. Three drivers: (a) how likely default is, (b) how much you're owed at that time, (c) how much you lose per default. --- ACCOUNTING: Under IFRS 13/ASC 820, CVA must be reflected in fair value of derivatives.
What is DVA?	DVA = Debt Value Adjustment = the PV of expected loss the COUNTERPARTY bears if YOU (the bank) default. --- PARADOX EXPLAINED: If your own bank defaults, you don't have to pay the counterparty in full. This "savings from your own default" is economically real — it reduces your obligations. So DVA is a BENEFIT added to your derivative value. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: (1) Mirror image of CVA but from the counterparty's perspective looking at YOUR credit risk. (2) As your own credit quality deteriorates, your DVA INCREASES (your default is more likely → bigger "benefit"). (3) This creates the perverse result: your own credit downgrade makes your P&L look BETTER via DVA gains. --- CONTROVERSY: Basel III largely disallows DVA benefit in regulatory capital because it's not a realizable cash gain — you can't monetize your own default.
What is the formula for credit-adjusted derivative value?	V(credit-adjusted) = V(no-default) − CVA + DVA. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: Start with the idealized risk-free value. (1) SUBTRACT CVA: the counterparty might default on you, so the derivative is worth less. CVA is always positive and always a cost. (2) ADD DVA: you might default on the counterparty, which is a "benefit" (you escape obligations). DVA is always positive and always a gain. --- NET EFFECT: For a bank with strong credit (low own-PD) facing a weak counterparty (high PD), CVA dominates → net adjustment is negative. For a weak bank facing a strong counterparty, DVA dominates → net adjustment could be positive. --- BILATERAL CVA: The difference (CVA − DVA) is sometimes called Bilateral CVA (BCVA), representing the net credit adjustment.
SIGN TRAP: In V = V(no-default) − CVA + DVA, which sign does CVA get and which does DVA get?	CVA is SUBTRACTED (−). DVA is ADDED (+). --- WHY STUDENTS GET THIS WRONG: Both CVA and DVA are defined as POSITIVE numbers (expected losses). The signs come from whose perspective you're on: CVA is a cost TO you (subtract). DVA is a cost to your counterparty, which is a benefit FOR you (add). --- MNEMONIC: "C Costs you, D Donates to you." Or: "CVA makes the price worse (−), DVA makes it better (+)." --- EXAM PATTERN: GARP often tests this by giving all three values and asking for the adjusted price. Common mistake: adding CVA or subtracting DVA.
What is wrong-way risk (WWR)?	WWR = counterparty's PD is POSITIVELY correlated with your EXPOSURE to them. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: (1) When market conditions cause the counterparty to become more likely to default, those SAME conditions increase what they owe you. (2) Peak exposure coincides with peak PD → worst possible combination. --- EXAMPLE: You buy a put option from a bank on its own stock. If the stock crashes, the put becomes very valuable (high exposure) AND the bank is closer to default (high PD). Both worsen simultaneously. --- IMPACT ON CVA: Standard CVA models assume PD and exposure are independent. WWR means the true CVA is HIGHER than the model estimates. Independence assumption UNDERESTIMATES risk. --- AIG CASE: AIG sold CDS protection on mortgage-backed securities. When mortgages defaulted, AIG's exposure exploded AND AIG itself neared default — textbook WWR.
What is right-way risk (RWR)?	RWR = counterparty's PD is NEGATIVELY correlated with your EXPOSURE to them. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: When the counterparty becomes riskier, your exposure to them naturally DECREASES. The risk and the exposure move in opposite directions — a natural hedge. --- EXAMPLE: An oil producer sells you a forward to deliver oil at a fixed price. If oil prices crash, the forward becomes valuable to you (high exposure), BUT the oil producer's revenues are now lower and their PD rises... WAIT — that's actually WWR. --- CORRECT EXAMPLE: You enter a commodity swap where rising commodity prices increase the counterparty's revenue AND increase what they owe you. No — let's be precise: RWR occurs when the counterparty's creditworthiness IMPROVES in the states where your exposure is highest. --- IMPACT ON CVA: RWR means standard CVA models OVERESTIMATE the true CVA. The natural hedge reduces joint expected loss.
A new trade is positively correlated with existing trades. What happens to CVA and DVA?	BOTH CVA AND DVA INCREASE. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: (1) Positive correlation means the new trade's exposure peaks at the same time as existing trades' exposures. (2) This concentrates exposure — instead of diversifying across different market scenarios, you're piling up exposure in the same states of the world. (3) Higher peak exposure × same PD = higher expected loss in both directions (counterparty defaults on you = CVA↑, you default on counterparty = DVA↑). --- PORTFOLIO EFFECT: This is the opposite of diversification. Netting helps when trades are negatively correlated (offsets). Positive correlation means netting benefits are smaller and gross exposure is closer to net exposure. --- RISK MANAGEMENT: This is why dealers try to have balanced books — long and short positions that offset, reducing both CVA and DVA.
What are the three standard credit risk mitigants for bilaterally cleared transactions?	THREE MITIGANTS: --- (1) NETTING: All trades under one ISDA are aggregated. Positive MtMs offset negative MtMs → net exposure can be dramatically lower than gross. Logic: netting converts many bilateral claims into one net claim, reducing bankruptcy complexity and exposure. --- (2) COLLATERAL (margin): Cash or securities posted to cover current exposure. Variation margin tracks daily MtM changes. Initial margin covers potential future exposure between margin calls. Logic: collateral converts unsecured exposure into secured exposure. --- (3) DOWNGRADE TRIGGERS: Contractual clauses requiring additional collateral or allowing termination if counterparty's rating falls below a threshold (e.g., below BBB). Logic: early warning system — exit before full default. --- ORDERING BY RELIABILITY: Netting (legal, automatic) > Collateral (contractual, continuous) > Triggers (conditional, can fail in systemic stress).
Three trades with a single counterparty: +$12M, −$4M, −$3M. What is the net exposure after netting?	NETTED EXPOSURE = $5M. --- STEP-BY-STEP: (1) Without netting (gross positive exposure): only the +$12M counts as exposure (negative MtM trades are your obligations, not your risk). Gross exposure = $12M. (2) With netting: $12M + (−$4M) + (−$3M) = $5M. If positive, this is your net exposure. If negative, your exposure is $0 (you owe them, so their default doesn't hurt you). (3) Exposure reduction from netting = $12M − $5M = $7M (58% reduction). --- WHY THIS WORKS: On counterparty default, ISDA close-out netting collapses all trades into one net number. The $4M and $3M you owe them effectively cancel against the $12M they owe you. Without the ISDA, you'd owe them $7M in full AND have a $12M unsecured claim in bankruptcy.
Why do downgrade triggers lose effectiveness in a broad credit deterioration?	TRIGGERS FAIL IN SYSTEMIC CRISES. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: (1) Downgrade triggers assume the defaulting firm is an IDIOSYNCRATIC event — one counterparty deteriorates while others remain healthy. (2) In systemic crises (2008, COVID), many counterparties get downgraded simultaneously. (3) Everyone tries to call triggers at once → mass unwinding, but there is no liquidity to absorb it. (4) The "insurance policy" of early termination expires worthless exactly when the whole market is in distress. --- AIG EXAMPLE: AIG's CDS contracts had triggers tied to its own credit rating. When AIG was downgraded, it triggered massive collateral calls it couldn't meet, ACCELERATING its collapse. The trigger didn't prevent loss — it amplified it. --- DEEPER ISSUE: Triggers create PROCYCLICAL behavior — they force actions (margin calls, terminations) at the worst possible time, worsening the crisis. --- EXAM ANGLE: Triggers are useful for idiosyncratic risk but unreliable for systematic risk. This distinction is frequently tested.
What are the three sources of default correlation?	THREE SOURCES: --- (1) SECTOR/REGION SHOCKS: Firms in the same industry or geography share risk factors. An oil price crash simultaneously stresses all energy firms. Logic: common exposure → correlated defaults. --- (2) ECONOMY-WIDE CYCLES: Recessions raise default rates across ALL sectors because aggregate demand falls, financing tightens, and asset values drop. Logic: the business cycle is a systematic factor that no firm can fully escape. --- (3) CONTAGION (direct linkage): One firm's default CAUSES another's — via supply chains (your biggest customer defaults → you lose revenue), financial linkages (interbank lending), or confidence effects (Lehman's failure → money market fund breaking the buck). Logic: not just common exposure but actual TRANSMISSION of distress from firm to firm. --- KEY DISTINCTION: Sources (1) and (2) are about shared EXPOSURE to common factors. Source (3) is about CAUSAL CHAINS — one default directly triggers another. Models handle (1) and (2) through factor correlations but struggle to capture (3).
Compare reduced-form vs structural models for default correlation.	TWO MODELING FAMILIES: --- REDUCED-FORM MODELS: (1) Mechanism: Each firm has a hazard rate λᵢ(t) that follows a stochastic process driven by shared macro factors. (2) Correlation source: Common macro factors (GDP, rates, spreads) drive co-movement in hazard rates. (3) Pros: Analytically tractable, calibrate well to market data, naturally capture credit cycles. (4) Limitation: The probability of two firms defaulting in the EXACT same short interval dt is approximately zero (even with correlated hazards). This creates a LOW CEILING on achievable default correlation — typically maxing out around 20-30%. --- STRUCTURAL (MERTON-TYPE) MODELS: (1) Mechanism: Each firm's asset value follows a correlated geometric Brownian motion. Default occurs when assets fall below the debt barrier. (2) Correlation source: Correlated asset-value processes (like correlated stock returns). (3) Pros: Can produce ARBITRARILY HIGH default correlations — if two firms' assets are perfectly correlated, they default together. (4) Limitation: Computationally expensive (Monte Carlo simulation), hard to calibrate, require asset-value assumptions that aren't directly observable. --- EXAM SUMMARY: Need high correlation → structural. Need tractability → reduced-form.
TRAP: "Reduced-form models allow high default correlations." True or false?	FALSE. --- WHY: In reduced-form models, defaults arrive as "jumps" in continuous-time intensity processes. Even when two firms' hazard rates are highly correlated, the probability of both firms' jump events occurring in the same tiny time interval is vanishingly small. Correlated hazard rates ≠ correlated default events. --- ANALOGY: Two people can have highly correlated chances of catching a cold (both go up in winter), but the probability they catch a cold on the EXACT same day is still low. Correlated risk ≠ simultaneous realization. --- CORRECT STATEMENT: STRUCTURAL models allow arbitrarily high default correlations because they model the continuous asset process directly — if two firms' assets hit the barrier together (perfectly correlated paths), they default together with certainty. --- EXAM PATTERN: This is a classic "sounds right but is wrong" distractor. The question exploits confusion between "correlated PDs" and "correlated default events."
What does the Gaussian copula transform into joint normal variables?	TIMES TO DEFAULT (τᵢ) — nothing else. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC (step by step): (1) Each firm i has a marginal survival curve giving P(τᵢ > T) for any horizon T. This comes from CDS/bond data. (2) For each firm, map its time-to-default through the inverse normal CDF: xᵢ = N⁻¹(Fᵢ(τᵢ)), where Fᵢ is the marginal CDF of τᵢ. (3) The key assumption: the vector (x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ) follows a multivariate standard normal distribution with correlation matrix Σ. (4) This is the "copula" — it links marginal distributions (each firm's standalone default timing) into a joint distribution (how firms default together). --- WHAT IT IS NOT: The copula does NOT transform PDs (those are scalars, not random variables). It does NOT transform recovery rates or spreads. It transforms the RANDOM VARIABLE "time until default" into a normal variable. --- WHY THIS MATTERS: The Gaussian copula was the standard model for pricing CDOs (2000s). Its failure to capture tail dependence (extreme co-movement) contributed to the 2008 crisis — it underestimated the probability of many firms defaulting simultaneously.
What is the one-factor Gaussian copula decomposition?	xᵢ = √ρ × F + √(1−ρ) × Zᵢ. --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: (1) xᵢ is the normalized time-to-default for firm i (from the copula transformation). (2) F ~ N(0,1) is the COMMON MARKET FACTOR — represents the state of the economy. Same F for ALL firms. (3) Zᵢ ~ N(0,1) is the FIRM-SPECIFIC SHOCK — independent of F and of all other Zⱼ. (4) √ρ and √(1−ρ) are weights that ensure Var(xᵢ) = ρ + (1−ρ) = 1 and Corr(xᵢ, xⱼ) = ρ. --- KEY INSIGHT: Conditional on knowing F (the economy's state), all firms' defaults become INDEPENDENT because the only remaining randomness is the independent Zᵢ. This is the "conditional independence" trick that makes the model tractable. --- INTERPRETATION OF ρ: ρ = fraction of default risk that is SYSTEMATIC (driven by the economy). (1−ρ) = fraction that is IDIOSYNCRATIC (firm-specific). High ρ → clustered defaults in bad economies. Low ρ → defaults are more spread out.
What is the conditional default probability formula in the one-factor copula?	Q(T|F) = N( [N⁻¹(Q(T)) − √ρ × F] / √(1−ρ) ). --- DERIVATION LOGIC: (1) Firm i defaults by time T if xᵢ ≤ N⁻¹(Q(T)), where Q(T) is the marginal PD. (2) Substitute xᵢ = √ρ F + √(1−ρ) Zᵢ. (3) Default condition becomes: √(1−ρ) Zᵢ ≤ N⁻¹(Q(T)) − √ρ F. (4) Divide by √(1−ρ): Zᵢ ≤ [N⁻¹(Q(T)) − √ρ F] / √(1−ρ). (5) Since Zᵢ ~ N(0,1), this probability = N([N⁻¹(Q(T)) − √ρ F] / √(1−ρ)). --- INTUITION: When F is very NEGATIVE (bad economy), the threshold for Zᵢ becomes very high → almost any firm-specific shock triggers default → conditional PD → 1. When F is very POSITIVE (good economy), threshold drops → conditional PD → 0. --- EXAM USE: This formula is the engine behind Credit VaR calculation. Set F to a stress quantile and compute the resulting default rate.
What is the Credit VaR formula using the Gaussian copula?	Credit VaR = L × (1 − RR) × V(T,X). --- COMPONENT BREAKDOWN: (1) L = total portfolio face value (e.g., $100M loan pool). (2) (1 − RR) = Loss Given Default (e.g., 1 − 0.40 = 0.60). (3) V(T,X) = Worst-Case Default Rate (WCDR) = the default rate at the X-th percentile of the common factor F. Derived from the conditional PD formula with F set to N⁻¹(1 − X). --- STRUCTURAL LOGIC: (1) In the one-factor model, conditional on F, defaults are independent. (2) By the law of large numbers, the ACTUAL default rate in a large portfolio converges to the conditional PD. (3) So the worst-case default rate at confidence X is just the conditional PD when the economy is at its X-th worst percentile. (4) Multiply by LGD and portfolio size to get dollar VaR. --- WHY IT WORKS: The copula converts a complex multi-name credit problem into a simple formula by exploiting conditional independence + law of large numbers. One common factor drives all the action.
Loan portfolio L = $100M, recovery RR = 40%, V(T,X) = 5%. What is the Credit VaR?	Credit VaR = $100M × 0.60 × 0.05 = $3,000,000. --- STEP-BY-STEP: (1) LGD = 1 − RR = 1 − 0.40 = 0.60 (lose 60 cents per dollar on default). (2) WCDR = V(T,X) = 5% (at the chosen confidence level, up to 5% of borrowers default). (3) Expected loss from defaults = $100M × 5% × 60% = $3M. --- INTERPRETATION: At the specified confidence level (e.g., 99.9%), the portfolio could lose up to $3M over the time horizon T. --- SCALING INTUITION: If recovery drops to 20%: VaR = $100M × 0.80 × 0.05 = $4M (33% higher). If WCDR doubles to 10%: VaR = $100M × 0.60 × 0.10 = $6M. All three inputs (L, LGD, WCDR) are multiplicative — small changes in any one can significantly move VaR.
How does CreditMetrics differ from the basic Gaussian copula Credit VaR approach?	CREDITMETRICS is RICHER but more COMPLEX. --- KEY DIFFERENCES: (1) SCOPE: Basic copula models only DEFAULT (binary: default or survive). CreditMetrics models full RATING TRANSITIONS (AAA→AA, AA→A, ..., → default). A bond downgraded from A to BBB loses value even without defaulting. (2) METHOD: CreditMetrics uses MONTE CARLO simulation, not closed-form. For each simulation: draw correlated asset returns → map to new ratings via thresholds from transition matrix → revalue portfolio at new ratings → aggregate P&L. (3) CORRELATION INPUT: CreditMetrics uses EQUITY return correlations as proxies for asset correlations (not bond or CDS correlations). Logic: equity prices are liquid, observable, and co-move with the firm's asset value (Merton model link: equity = call option on assets). (4) COPULA ROLE: The Gaussian copula links the asset returns across firms, just like in the basic model, but now the thresholds define RATING TRANSITIONS, not just default. --- EXAM ANGLE: "CreditMetrics models rating migrations; the basic copula only models default." This is the single most testable distinction.
