# R95 — Distress Remedies

**Exam Priority:** 🟡 Medium (1-2 questions) and Remedies

## 1. Foundational Propositions
| ID | Proposition | Mapping | Intuition |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| `P.95.1` | **The Sale-Leaseback Remedy:** A distressed firm sells an asset (like a headquarters) for immediate cash and leases it back. This improves liquidity while maintaining operational use of the asset. | `[OPS/THE]` | You sell your car to a friend and pay them to let you keep driving it. You get the cash now to pay your bills, and you still get to work. |
| `P.95.2` | **OCR vs. ICR (Holdout Risk):** Out-of-court restructurings (OCR) are faster and cheaper but vulnerable to "Holdouts"—creditors who refuse the deal hoping for a better payout. In-court restructurings (ICR/Bankruptcy) can force a deal on everyone but are slow and damage the brand. | `[REG/OPS]` | OCR is a "Handshake deal." If one person won't shake, the deal fails. ICR is a "Court Order." The judge makes everyone shake hands, whether they want to or not. |
| `P.95.3` | **Early Detection (Creative Accounting):** Financial distress is often preceded by "Creative Accounting" such as frequent changes in fiscal year-end or the use of earnings enhancements to mask operational failure. | `[OPS/REG]` | If a company keeps changing "the rules of the game" (Accounting policies), it’s usually because they are losing and don't want you to see the score. |
| `P.95.4` | **Open Market Repurchases:** Effective only for reducing **small amounts of debt** (<20%). They are fast and discreet. For large debt reduction, formal tender offers are required. | `[MTH/OPS]` | If you want to buy back a few shares without drawing attention, you do it on the quiet. If you want to buy half the company, you have to make a "Public Announcement." |
| `P.95.5` | **Restructuring Constraints:** The most common hurdle is **Limited Liquidity**. Paradoxically, you need cash to execute a plan to save a firm that is running out of cash (e.g., to buy back debt at a discount). | `[ECO/OPS]` | It "Costs money to save money." If you are completely broke, you can't even afford the lawyer or the cash needed to buy back your own cheap debt. |

## 2. Constraint Stress-Tests (Falsification)
- **Variable Flip: If a company has only "Core" assets (no separable units):** Its restructuring options are **Severely Limited**. It cannot raise cash by selling "non-essential" pieces without killing its main business.
- **Variable Flip: If a firm faces an "Unexpected Liability" (e.g., a massive lawsuit):** This can trigger sudden distress even if the core business and leverage were perfectly managed.
- **Variable Flip: If a firm has a "Crisis of Confidence" due to fraud:** Even if it has good assets, it might face an immediate **Liquidity Run** as creditors and partners withdraw all support simultaneously.

## 3. Dependency & Noise Map
| Signal (Exam Core) | Noise (Distractors) |
|:---|:---|
| **OCR:** Fast, cheap, voluntary negotiation. | **OCR:** Eliminates holdout risk (NO: it creates it). |
| **ICR:** Court-ordered stay, class-based voting. | **ICR:** Preserves reputation (NO: it usually damages it). |
| **Sale-Leaseback:** Improves liquidity, maintains use. | **Asset Sale:** Maintains use (NO: you lose the asset). |

## 4. Directional Intuition
- **Complexity of Capital Structure $\uparrow \rightarrow$ Probability of OCR Success $\downarrow$:** The more different types of creditors you have, the harder it is to get them all to agree on a voluntary deal.
- **Debt Price Discount $\uparrow \rightarrow$ Potential Gain from Repurchase $\uparrow$:** If your debt trades at $0.30 on the dollar, buying it back is the most efficient way to "erase" your liabilities—if you have the cash.
- **Restructuring Timeline $\uparrow \rightarrow$ Value Recovery $\downarrow$:** The longer a firm stays in "Distress Mode," the more customers leave and the more legal fees eat up the remaining assets.

## 5. Ambiguity Traps (Anti-Decoder)
- **Financial vs. Nonfinancial:** 
    - Covenant breach = **Financial**. 
    - Change in fiscal year-end = **Nonfinancial**.
- **The "Holdout":** Why are they dangerous? Because if one big creditor holds out, the company might be forced into costly **Chapter 11** bankruptcy.
- **Sale-Leaseback Accounting:** Note that these often require "Analytical Adjustments" because they can hide debt as an operating lease (Off-balance sheet).
- **Core vs. Non-core:** A "Roll-up" strategy (buying many small companies) often fails because it creates a complex, disjointed company that is hard to restructure.
- **Stay on Claims:** This is the "Shield" of the court that keeps creditors from seizing assets while the firm tries to reorganize.
- **Upstream Guarantees:** A legal risk where a subsidiary's guarantee of its parent's debt might be challenged in court as a "fraudulent transfer."
- **Equity Sponsor:** A private equity owner who can potentially save the firm by injecting more "Junior" capital.
- **Accounting Smells:** Changes in auditors or late submissions are the "Smoke" before the "Fire" of bankruptcy.
